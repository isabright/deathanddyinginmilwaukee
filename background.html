Death Taboo

Death is a long-standing taboo in western societies that speakers often avoid discussing directly both out of fear and as a show of politeness. In certain contexts, however, the subject of death is unavoidable. Obituaries, announcing the death of an individual within and community, are a place where the fact of a person’s death is unavoidable. Even still, people do not often say the words "death" or "died" outright,. Instead, they opt for euphemistic metaphors that relate death to some other quality, usually positive.


Theories of Metaphor

Lakoff and Johnson's (1980) Conceptual Metaphor Theory (CMT) provides a strong basis to understand the euphemistic metaphors present in obituaries. According to CMT, metaphor is a way to understand one domain (the target domain – death in this study) in terms of a different domain (the source domain). For example, when we use the phrase, "rest in peace", this allows us to understand the target domain of death by means of the source domains REST and PEACE , seeing the deceased not as dead, but rather in a tranquil slumber


Steen's (2015, 2017) Deliberate Metaphor Theory (DMT) builds off of this by claiming metaphor is not merely a linguistic process but also involves both thought and communication. Under DMT, understanding what metaphors people use (what the target and source domains are) can help us understand how people think about concept and how they attempt to communicate these thoughts with others.


Past Studies

Previous work has been done on the types of metaphors used in epitaphs (engravings on headstones). Crespo-Fernández (2011, 2023) collected and analyzed epitaphs in London, England, and Málaga, Spain to understand the variety of metaphors used and what that may indicate about how the writers of epitaphs conceptualize death. From these studies, he found that in the Highgate Cemetery in London, DEATH IS A JOURNEY and DEATH IS SLEEP/REST are the most common metaphors, and in English Cemetery of Málaga, DEATH IS SLEEP/REST and DEATH IS PEACE are the most common.
